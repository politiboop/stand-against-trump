The partisan gap in economic performance is one of the most robust findings in political economy. It has been documented by Princeton economists, published in the American Economic Review, and verified against 75 years of government data from the BEA, BLS, Treasury, and CBO.

The debate among economists is not about whether the gap exists. It does. The debate is about why.

---

## GDP Growth

Average annual real GDP growth since WWII (Bureau of Economic Analysis data, compiled in Blinder & Watson 2016, *American Economic Review*):

- **Democratic presidents: 4.33%**
- **Republican presidents: 2.54%**

The gap — 1.79 percentage points — is large, persistent, and statistically significant. It holds across different time periods and different measures.

By president:

| President | Party | Avg. Annual GDP Growth |
|-----------|-------|----------------------|

| Eisenhower | R | ~2.5% |
| Kennedy/Johnson | D | ~4.6-5.0% |
| Nixon | R | ~2.7% |
| Ford | R | ~2.2% |
| Carter | D | ~3.3% |
| Reagan | R | ~3.5% |
| G.H.W. Bush | R | ~2.2% |
| Clinton | D | ~3.9% |
| G.W. Bush | R | ~1.6% |
| Obama | D | ~2.0% |
| Trump (1st term) | R | ~1.0% |

Reagan is the strongest Republican performer. He's still below the Democratic average.

## Job Creation

The gap in job creation is even wider than GDP. Bureau of Labor Statistics nonfarm payroll data:

| President | Party | Total Jobs Created |
|-----------|-------|--------------------|
| Truman | D | ~8.4 million |
| Eisenhower | R | ~3.5 million |
| Kennedy/Johnson | D | ~15.7 million |
| Nixon/Ford | R | ~11.2 million |
| Carter | D | ~10.5 million |
| Reagan | R | ~16.1 million |
| G.H.W. Bush | R | ~2.6 million |
| Clinton | D | ~22.9 million |
| G.W. Bush | R | ~1.3 million |
| Obama | D | ~11.6 million |
| Trump (1st term) | R | ~-2.7 million |
| Biden | D | ~16.6 million |

Average monthly job creation:
- **Democratic presidents: ~150,000-160,000/month**
- **Republican presidents: ~60,000-70,000/month**

The Clinton vs. George W. Bush comparison is the most striking: 22.9 million jobs vs. 1.3 million. Clinton left a surplus. Bush left the Great Recession.

Trump's net-negative number reflects COVID. Pre-COVID, his pace (~187,000/month through February 2020) was solid but slightly slower than Obama's final three years (~225,000/month from 2014-2016).

## Stock Market

The S&P 500 has performed significantly better under Democratic presidents. This has been documented by Jeremy Siegel (Wharton) and multiple financial research firms:

- **Democratic presidents: ~10-11% annualized return**
- **Republican presidents: ~5-7% annualized return**

Best performers: Clinton (~17%), Obama (~16%), Reagan (~14%), Biden (~14% through 2024).

Worst performers: George W. Bush (negative total return over 8 years), Nixon (negative due to 1973-74 crash).

This is sometimes called the "presidential puzzle" in financial economics — it contradicts the assumption that markets prefer Republican pro-business policies.

## Deficits and Debt

Despite branding themselves as the party of fiscal responsibility, Republican presidents have presided over larger increases in the national debt as a share of GDP.

**Clinton:** Turned a $290 billion deficit into a $236 billion surplus. Debt-to-GDP fell from 49% to 33%. The last four years saw budget surpluses — the first since 1969.

**George W. Bush:** Turned Clinton's surplus into a $1.4 trillion deficit. Unfunded tax cuts, two unfunded wars, and unfunded Medicare expansion. Debt-to-GDP rose from 33% to 52%.

**Obama:** Inherited the $1.4 trillion deficit. Cut it by more than half to $585 billion by FY 2016. Total debt grew due to running deficits throughout, but the trajectory was improvement.

**Trump (1st term):** Produced $1 trillion+ deficits *before* COVID — during peacetime, at full employment. The FY 2019 deficit was $984 billion with no crisis to justify it. COVID pushed FY 2020 to $3.1 trillion. Debt-to-GDP rose from 77% to 100%.

**Reagan:** Tripled the national debt in nominal terms. The 1981 tax cuts produced massive structural deficits. Debt-to-GDP rose from 26% to 41%.

The nonpartisan Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget has documented that the Reagan, Bush, and Trump tax cuts are the single largest drivers of structural deficits — larger than any spending programs.

| Recession | President | Party |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 1948-49 | Truman | D |
| 1953 | Eisenhower | R |
| 1957-58 | Eisenhower | R |
| 1960-61 | Eisenhower | R |
| 1969-70 | Nixon | R |
| 1973-75 | Nixon | R |
| 1980 | Carter | D |
| 1981-82 | Reagan | R |
| 1990-91 | G.H.W. Bush | R |
| 2001 | G.W. Bush | R |
| 2007-09 | G.W. Bush | R |
| 2020 | Trump | R |

The Carter recession was induced by Fed Chair Volcker's rate hikes to break inflation. The 2001 recession began two months into Bush's presidency (roots predated him). The 2020 recession was caused by COVID. Even accounting for all caveats, the pattern is 10-2.

## Income: Who Benefits?

This may be the most important finding.

Princeton political scientist Larry Bartels documented in *Unequal Democracy* (2008, updated 2016) that income growth under Democratic and Republican presidents follows fundamentally different patterns:

- **Under Democrats:** The bottom 20% of earners see the fastest income growth (~2.6%/year). Growth is shared broadly across the income spectrum.
- **Under Republicans:** The top 20% see the fastest growth. The bottom 20% see near-zero real income growth (~0.4%/year).
- **The top 20% does roughly the same under either party** (~1.6%/year).

The economy doesn't just grow faster under Democrats. The gains are distributed more broadly. Under Republicans, growth concentrates at the top.

Real median household income grew approximately $6,000 under Clinton, stagnated under George W. Bush, recovered under Obama's second term, and reached record levels under Biden (though perception lagged because cumulative price increases from 2021-2022 inflation were still felt).

## The Counter-Arguments

The data above is not in dispute among economists. The debate is about what it means.

### "Presidents don't control the economy"

Partly true. The Fed sets interest rates. Global events drive commodity prices. Technological shifts create growth independent of policy. But presidents sign tax and spending legislation, appoint Fed chairs, set regulatory policy, and negotiate trade deals. They are not irrelevant.

### "Lag effects — credit the previous president"

Blinder & Watson tested for lags extensively. Even crediting the previous president's policies with a 1-2 year delay, the Democratic advantage persists. It narrows slightly but does not disappear.

### "It's about Congress, not the president"

Multiple studies have tested this. Presidential party is a better predictor of economic outcomes than congressional party. The Democratic advantage holds whether Congress is controlled by Democrats, Republicans, or split.

### "Democrats just get lucky"

This is actually the Blinder & Watson conclusion. They attributed much of the gap to oil price shocks, productivity trends, and global conditions that were more favorable under Democrats. They called it "mostly good luck." But they also noted: the question of *why Democrats consistently get luckier* remains open. And luck doesn't explain the Bartels income distribution finding — policy choices about who benefits from growth are not random.

### "The economy was going to recover anyway"

Democrats often inherit recessions (because Republicans often create them). The recovery might have happened under anyone. This is a fair point — but it concedes that Republican presidencies tend to end with recessions. And the speed and equity of recovery do vary based on policy (the post-2008 recovery was slowed by Republican-pushed austerity measures like sequestration).

### "Republican policies benefit long-term growth"

Supply-side economists argue that tax cuts boost potential GDP growth in ways not captured by these comparisons. The Tax Foundation and similar organizations contend that marginal tax rate reductions increase incentives to work and invest. The counterfactual (what would have happened without the cuts) is unknowable. However, 75 years of data across 14 presidents is a substantial sample, and the supply-side predictions of self-financing tax cuts have not materialized in any instance.

## The Princeton Study

The landmark academic study is Blinder & Watson (2016), published in the *American Economic Review* — the most prestigious economics journal. The authors are Princeton economists, not partisan commentators.

Their findings: the 1.79 percentage point GDP gap is real, large, and statistically significant. They tested every objection — lag effects, congressional control, initial conditions, oil prices, external shocks — and the gap persisted across all specifications.

Their conclusion was cautious: the gap is "mostly good luck" rather than clearly attributable to policy. But they noted this conclusion is uncertain, and the consistency of the Democratic advantage across decades raises questions about whether it is truly random.

Bartels' complementary finding — that income growth is more equitable under Democrats — is harder to attribute to luck. Tax policy, minimum wage policy, labor market regulation, and social spending are policy choices that directly affect who benefits from economic growth. These choices differ systematically by party, and the outcomes match the predictions.
